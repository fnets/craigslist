              data-track-label="http://feeds.feedblitz.com/~/152606878/0/songsyouresickof/zqeg~Rock-You-Like-A-Hurricane/"
              target="_blank">
              “Rock You Like A Hurricane”
            </a>
          </span>
        </h4>
        <div class="podmass-entry-image">
          <figure>
    <div data-type="image"  class="image crop-1x1" data-image-id="580947">
        <div>
            <noscript>
                <img src="http://i.onionstatic.com/avclub/5809/47/1x1/150.jpg" >
            </noscript>
        </div>
    </div>

    

    
</figure>
          
          <a 
            class="itunes-link" 
            href="https://itunes.apple.com/us/podcast/songs-youre-sick-of/id998398622?mt=2&amp;uo=4&amp;at=10lPNj" 
            data-track-action="Podmass Article: iTunes"
            data-track-label="https://itunes.apple.com/us/podcast/songs-youre-sick-of/id998398622?mt=2&amp;uo=4&amp;at=10lPNj"
            target="_blank">
            <img src="//assets3.onionstatic.com/onionstatic/avclub/static/core/img/itunes-subscribe.svg">
          </a>
          
        </div>
        <div class="podmass-entry-text">
          <p>There are a lot of songs that a lot of people have deep-down bone-marrow familiarity with despite having never once actively chosen to listen to them. They simply enter the consciousness through a process of cultural osmosis and burrow themselves into the crevices of our psyche where they are largely immune from criticism. <i>Songs You’re Sick Of</i> is a podcast that makes its business of dragging such songs out into the light where they can be properly examined. In this episode, host Joe Dator and guest host Susan Kruglinski spend a full half hour dissecting, via playful and sometimes rambling conversation, Scorpions’ 1984 hit “Rock You Like A Hurricane.” One of the things they discover is that while this ode to aggressive sex is the German band’s first major U.S. hit, it actually comes from their ninth studio album—the band formed two decades earlier in 1965. Another thing: The band’s uncomfortable history of misogyny, which resulted in one album cover that should definitely not be Googled by law-abiding citizens. Billy Joel’s “<a href="http://songsyouresickof.com/blog/2016/04/26/its-still-rock-and-roll-to-me/" target="_blank">It’s Still Rock And Roll To Me</a>” and Toto’s “<a href="http://songsyouresickof.com/blog/2016/04/18/africa/" target="_blank">Africa</a>” receive similar treatment in earlier installments.</p>
        </div>
        <div class="podmass-entry-author">
          [<span class="author"><a class="author" 
	href="/author/DennisDiClaudio/"
	data-track-action="Podmass Article: Author"
	data-track-label="/author/DennisDiClaudio/">Dennis DiClaudio</a></span>]
        </div>

      <div class="podmass-entry">
        <h4>
          <span class="podmass-entry-podcast">
            This American Life
          </span>
          <br>
          <span class="podmass-entry-episode">
            <a 
              href="http://feed.thisamericanlife.org/~r/talpodcast/~3/5E3GseAwB0U/prom" 
              data-track-action="Podmass Article: Episode"
              data-track-label="http://feed.thisamericanlife.org/~r/talpodcast/~3/5E3GseAwB0U/prom"
              target="_blank">
              Prom
            </a>
          </span>
        </h4>
        <div class="podmass-entry-image">
          <figure>
    <div data-type="image"  class="image crop-1x1" data-image-id="533463">
        <div>
            <noscript>
                <img src="http://i.onionstatic.com/avclub/5334/63/1x1/150.jpg" >
            </noscript>
        </div>
    </div>

    

    
</figure>
          
          <a 
            class="itunes-link" 
            href="https://itunes.apple.com/us/podcast/this-american-life/id201671138?mt=2&amp;uo=4&amp;at=10lPNj" 
            data-track-action="Podmass Article: iTunes"
            data-track-label="https://itunes.apple.com/us/podcast/this-american-life/id201671138?mt=2&amp;uo=4&amp;at=10lPNj"
            target="_blank">
            <img src="//assets3.onionstatic.com/onionstatic/avclub/static/core/img/itunes-subscribe.svg">
          </a>
          
        </div>
        <div class="podmass-entry-text">
          <p>Prom has permeated our culture in such a universal way that it’s hardly ever looked at with an artfully specific eye. Instead, it’s often deemed an evening involving tipsy teens awkwardly slow dancing in gaudy outfits, culminating in nothing more than a notch in one’s cultural belt. This week’s episode of <i>This American Life</i>, however,<i> </i>focuses on stories where prom changes lives. The first act follows the story of the small town of Hoisington, Kansas, where in 2001, “a night to remember” was redefined, as unbeknownst to attendees, a tornado ripped through one-third of the town. The teens struggle to conceptualize why the tornado hit when and where it did, if it was fate that they were kept in the dark, and how the storm taught everyone a lesson in a town where “nothing ever happened.” The episode ends by providing contrast with a story of Racine, Wisconsin, where prom includes parades, red carpets, and its own TV program. Revealing carefully how much of the hype surrounding prom relies on the restless feeling that it’s the night where anything can happen, <i>This American Life</i> truly captures the moment. </p>
        </div>
        <div class="podmass-entry-author">
          [<span class="author"><a class="author" 
	href="/author/rebeccabulnez/"
	data-track-action="Podmass Article: Author"
	data-track-label="/author/rebeccabulnez/">Rebecca Bulnes</a></span>]
        </div>

      <div class="podmass-entry">
        <h4>
          <span class="podmass-entry-podcast">
            Trumpcast
          </span>
          <br>
          <span class="podmass-entry-episode">
            <a 
              href="https://itunes.apple.com/us/podcast/slates-trumpcast/id1093260018?mt=2&amp;uo=4&amp;at=10lPNj" 
              data-track-action="Podmass Article: Episode"
              data-track-label="https://itunes.apple.com/us/podcast/slates-trumpcast/id1093260018?mt=2&amp;uo=4&amp;at=10lPNj"
              target="_blank">
              A Man Of His Generation
            </a>
          </span>
        </h4>
        <div class="podmass-entry-image">
          <figure>
    <div data-type="image"  class="image crop-1x1" data-image-id="575385">
        <div>
            <noscript>
                <img src="http://i.onionstatic.com/avclub/5753/85/1x1/150.jpg" >
            </noscript>

</figure>
          
          <a 
            class="itunes-link" 
            href="https://itunes.apple.com/us/podcast/slates-trumpcast/id1093260018?mt=2&amp;uo=4&amp;at=10lPNj" 
            data-track-action="Podmass Article: iTunes"
            data-track-label="https://itunes.apple.com/us/podcast/slates-trumpcast/id1093260018?mt=2&amp;uo=4&amp;at=10lPNj"
            target="_blank">
            <img src="//assets3.onionstatic.com/onionstatic/avclub/static/core/img/itunes-subscribe.svg">
          </a>

        <div class="podmass-entry-text">
          <p><i>Slate</i>’s editor-in-chief Jacob Weisberg often makes the point that it is his sincere hope that the Donald-centric podcast he hosts several times a week is short-lived. Multiple times after the past few months, based on the conventional wisdom of pollsters and pundits, he’s happily reported that the existence of <i>Trumpcast</i> might soon lose its necessity. Unfortunately for him, and for everybody else in America, it appears that he will continue pumping out episodes into the November. Now, with Trump stands triumphant atop the Republican mountain with all of his vanquished foes heads at his feet, Weisberg welcomes Slate’s critic-at-large, Stephen Metcalf, to discuss his recent cover story “<a href="http://www.slate.com/articles/news_and_politics/cover_story/2016/05/donald_trump_baby_boomer_how_the_candidate_was_shaped_by_his_generation.html" target="_blank">Donald Trump, Baby Boomer</a>” to better understand how the real estate mogul is an almost cartoonishly spot-on manifestation of everything that is ugly and excessive about the 1980s’ philosophy of me. It’s illuminating to learn what effect the war in Vietnam had on Trump’s worldview and business practices, despite the multiple student deferments he received. Be forewarned though, this podcast is not wrestling with the question of Trump. Its mind was very much made up in its first episode back in March. This is best listened to by people who can’t help but fiddle with open sores.</p>
        </div>
        <div class="podmass-entry-author">
          [<span class="author"><a class="author" 
	href="/author/DennisDiClaudio/"
	data-track-action="Podmass Article: Author"

      <div class="podmass-entry">
        <h4>
          <span class="podmass-entry-podcast">
            With Special Guest Lauren Lapkus
          </span>
          <br>
          <span class="podmass-entry-episode">
            <a 
              href="http://www.earwolf.com/episode/chris-gethard-anonymous-stories-from-beautiful-people/" 
              data-track-action="Podmass Article: Episode"
              data-track-label="http://www.earwolf.com/episode/chris-gethard-anonymous-stories-from-beautiful-people/"
              target="_blank">
              Chris Gethard: <i>Anonymous Stories From Beautiful People</i>
            </a>
          </span>
        </h4>
        <div class="podmass-entry-image">
          <figure>
    <div data-type="image"  class="image crop-1x1" data-image-id="529544">
        <div>
            <noscript>
                <img src="http://i.onionstatic.com/avclub/5295/44/1x1/150.jpg" >
            </noscript>
        </div>
    </div>

    

    
</figure>
          
          <a 
            class="itunes-link" 
            href="https://itunes.apple.com/us/podcast/special-guest-lauren-lapkus/id944042911?mt=2&amp;uo=4&amp;at=10lPNj" 
            data-track-action="Podmass Article: iTunes"
            data-track-label="https://itunes.apple.com/us/podcast/special-guest-lauren-lapkus/id944042911?mt=2&amp;uo=4&amp;at=10lPNj"
            target="_blank">
            <img src="//assets3.onionstatic.com/onionstatic/avclub/static/core/img/itunes-subscribe.svg">
          </a>
          
        </div>
        <div class="podmass-entry-text">
          <p>That neither Lapkus nor the temporary host know what to expect is what makes <i>With Special Guest Lauren Lapkus</i> exciting. The genuine unpredictability of this week’s episode, for example, finds Chris Gethard hosting his actual Earwolf show <i>Beautiful Stories from Anonymous People</i> (slightly altered into <i>Anonymous Stories From Beautiful People</i>), with Lapkus acting as the anonymous caller. Gethard plays the entire episode with the utmost sincerity. Lapkus plays a caller whose father left home because of, as it’s slowly revealed, the many convoluted pranks her character put him through. As in actual episodes of the show, Gethard engages with the caller intimately and hardly ever makes it about himself, which allows him to push Lapkus for details, prompting her to come up with increasingly ridiculous specifics about the torment she put her father through. With every absurd reveal, Gethard masterfully navigates the improv to allow Lapkus to shine, reveling in the most unbelievable of storylines, while he remains the perfect, grounded foil. His ability to play the show straight ends up being equally as hilarious as Lapkus’ elaborately sadistic character.</p>
        </div>
